Our Aim



The Issue

Anaphylaxis is a life-threatening allergic reaction that can be triggered by specific allergens. Australia has one of the highest reported incidences of food allergies in the world, and the numbers are growing at an alarming rate (Allergy and Anaphylaxis Australia, 2016). A recent Australian study has confirmed that 10% of babies at age 12 months will have food allergies (Vale et al., 2015).


An adrenaline auto-injector delivers a life-saving dose of adrenaline in the case of an anaphylactic attack. However, this is only possible if the patient is carrying the device. Many people do not carry their auto-injectors due to a number of factors, including its size and the surrounding stigma (Money et al., 2013).


Our aim is to develop an adrenaline auto-injector device to supplement the Epipen; the Mini-Epi. The Mini-Epi is a compact pocket-sized adrenaline auto-injector that is designed to integrate into the daily lives of allergy sufferers as would other items such as a phone, key set or wallet.


What sets us apart is that we are a not-for-profit organisation, not another pharmaceutical company. And profits will go towards allergy research in Australia and health promotion for people with food allergies, other allergies, asthma, allergic rhinitis (hayfever) and eczema.
